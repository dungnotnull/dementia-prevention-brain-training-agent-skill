## 1. Core Concepts & Foundational Theory

### 1.1 Neuroplasticity
Neuroplasticity is the brain's capacity to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. Key principles:

- **Hebb's Rule (1949):** "Neurons that fire together, wire together." Repeated co-activation of synapses strengthens them — the cellular basis for learning-based cognitive training.
- **Use-Dependent Plasticity:** Brain regions that are used frequently undergo structural (gray matter volume) and functional (connectivity) changes. Cognitive training leverages this.
- **Adult Neurogenesis:** New neurons are generated in the hippocampus (dentate gyrus) throughout adult life. Aerobic exercise and cognitive novelty are the two most robust inducers.
- **Synaptic Pruning:** Unused connections are eliminated — cognitive disuse accelerates this loss in aging. Structured training prevents pruning of high-value circuits.

### 1.2 Dementia & Alzheimer's Disease Pathophysiology
- **Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis:** Abnormal accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques disrupts synaptic signaling; tau tangles cause neuronal death. Amyloid begins accumulating 15-20 years before symptom onset.
- **Default Mode Network (DMN) Vulnerability:** The DMN (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, hippocampus) is particularly vulnerable to early Alzheimer's pathology and particularly responsive to cognitive engagement interventions.
- **Cholinergic Deficit:** Loss of cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain (nucleus basalis of Meynert) is a core feature — explaining why social/novelty stimulation (which drives ACh release) is protective.
- **Vascular Contributions (VCID):** Vascular factors (hypertension, diabetes, obesity) cause white matter lesions that compound neurodegeneration. Cardiovascular risk management is integral to prevention.

### 1.3 Cognitive Reserve Theory
- **Stern (2002), Neurology:** Cognitive reserve (CR) is the brain's resilience against pathology. Individuals with higher CR (more education, richer occupational history, greater leisure cognitive activity) show later symptom onset despite equivalent pathology burden.
- **Passive vs. Active Reserve:** Passive CR = brain size/synapse count. Active CR = ability to recruit alternate neural networks when primary ones are damaged. Cognitive training builds active reserve.
- **Implication for Training:** Even after midlife, enriching cognitive engagement builds reserve. High-CR individuals show 60% lower risk of dementia onset (Valenzuela & Sachdev, 2006, Psychological Medicine).

### 1.4 Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF)
- BDNF is the primary neurotrophin supporting hippocampal plasticity, long-term potentiation, and neurogenesis.
- Aerobic exercise (especially moderate-intensity continuous training, MICT, 3x/week, 45 min) increases serum and hippocampal BDNF by 20-30% (Erickson et al., 2011, PNAS).
- BDNF levels predict hippocampal volume and episodic memory performance in older adults.
- Implication: Physical exercise must be integrated with cognitive training — their combined effect on BDNF is synergistic.

## 3. State-of-the-Art Methods & Tools

### 3.1 Validated Cognitive Training Approaches

#### Working Memory Training — N-Back Tasks
- **Protocol:** Dual n-back task (visual + auditory stimuli); start at 1-back, progress to 2-back (week 5-8), then 3-back (week 9-12)
- **Evidence:** Meta-analysis (Melby-Lervag et al., 2016, Psychological Bulletin) — near-transfer effects confirmed; far-transfer debated
- **Session duration:** 15-20 min, 3-5x/week
- **Mechanism:** Trains dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and parietal cortex network

#### Episodic Memory — Method of Loci
- **Protocol:** Visualize a familiar route (home to work); associate each memory item with a landmark along the route in vivid, unusual detail; mentally walk the route to retrieve items
- **Evidence:** Dresler et al. (2017, Neuron) — 72-hour word-list recall improved 97%; associated with hippocampal-cortical connectivity changes
- **Best for:** Name-face learning, grocery lists, appointment recall
- **Session duration:** 10-15 min, 3x/week

#### Speed-of-Processing Training (BrainHQ)
- **Protocol:** Visual discrimination tasks requiring rapid responses; adaptive difficulty
- **Evidence:** ACTIVE study — speed-of-processing training reduced functional decline by 33% over 10 years
- **Validated for:** IADLs (instrumental activities of daily living) — driving, reading, managing finances
- **Session duration:** 10 min, 4-5x/week

#### Dual-Task Training
- **Protocol:** Combine a physical task (walking, cycling) with a concurrent cognitive task (counting backward by 7s, naming categories)
- **Evidence:** Beurskens & Bock (2012); Eggenberger et al. (2015, BMC Geriatrics) — superior to single-task cognitive training for functional outcomes
- **Session duration:** 20-30 min (full dual-task session), 2-3x/week

#### Social Cognitive Engagement — Discussion & Reminiscence
- **Protocol:** Group-based discussions, reminiscence therapy, storytelling, teaching others
- **Evidence:** Kelly et al. (2017) — 38% risk reduction; Fratiglioni et al. (2004, Lancet Neurology) — social network density inversely correlated with dementia incidence
- **Frequency:** Minimum 3 social engagements/week

#### Language & Verbal Fluency Exercises
- **Protocol:** Category fluency tasks (name as many animals/fruits in 60 seconds); crossword puzzles; learning new vocabulary; bilingual conversation practice
- **Mechanism:** Preserves semantic network integrity; bilingualism delays dementia onset by ~5 years (Bialystok et al., 2007, Neuropsychologia)

#### Visuospatial Training
- **Protocol:** Map-drawing from memory; route navigation tasks; jigsaw puzzles; mental rotation exercises
- **Target domains:** Hippocampal spatial mapping; parietal lobe visuospatial processing
- **Evidence:** Spatial navigation training improves hippocampal structure (Woollett & Maguire, 2011, Current Biology — London taxi driver study)

### 3.2 Physical Exercise Protocols for Neuroplasticity
- **Type:** Moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (walking, cycling, swimming) preferred
- **Dose:** 150 min/week (WHO recommendation) — 3x 50-min sessions or 5x 30-min sessions
- **Intensity:** 50-70% maximum heart rate for older adults (calculated: 220 - age × 0.6)
- **Mechanism:** BDNF increase → hippocampal neurogenesis → episodic memory improvement
- **Optimal combination:** Aerobic exercise immediately before or during cognitive training maximizes BDNF-mediated learning (Hase et al., 2015, Frontiers in Human Neuroscience)

### 3.3 MIND Diet (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay)
**15 dietary components (Morris et al., 2015):**

Protective foods (aim for):
1. Green leafy vegetables — 6+ servings/week
2. Other vegetables — 1+ serving/day
3. Berries — 2+ servings/week (especially blueberries, strawberries)
4. Nuts — 5+ servings/week
5. Olive oil — primary cooking oil
6. Whole grains — 3+ servings/day
7. Fish — 1+ serving/week
8. Beans — 4+ meals/week
9. Poultry — 2+ servings/week
10. Wine — up to 1 glass/day (optional)

Foods to limit:
11. Red meat — < 4 servings/week
12. Butter/margarine — < 1 tbsp/day
13. Cheese — < 1 serving/week
14. Pastries/sweets — < 5 servings/week
15. Fried/fast food — < 1 serving/week

**Evidence:** High MIND diet adherence reduces Alzheimer's risk by 53%; moderate adherence reduces risk by 35%.

### 3.4 Sleep & Amyloid Clearance
- **Mechanism:** Glymphatic system (activated during slow-wave sleep) clears amyloid-beta and tau from the brain (Xie et al., 2013, Science)
- **Target:** 7-9 hours sleep for adults 65+ (National Sleep Foundation)
- **Key behaviors:** Consistent sleep-wake schedule; avoid alcohol within 3 hours of sleep; minimize blue light 2 hours before bed; cool, dark, quiet environment
- **Clinical note:** Chronic sleep deprivation (< 6 hours) is associated with 30% higher amyloid accumulation (Ju et al., 2017, Annals of Neurology)

## 5. Analytical Frameworks

### 5.1 Clinical Assessment Instruments
| Framework | Full Name | Purpose | Key Details |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| MMSE | Mini-Mental State Examination | Global cognitive screening | 30-point scale; ≤ 23 = cognitive impairment |
| MoCA | Montreal Cognitive Assessment | Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) screening | 30-point scale; ≥ 26 = normal; 18-25 = MCI range; < 18 = dementia range |
| ADAS-Cog | Alzheimer's Disease Assessment Scale - Cognitive | Clinical trial endpoint | 0-70 scale; higher = worse; gold standard for dementia trials |

| Clock Drawing Test | CDT | Visuospatial/executive function screening | 5-min test; sensitive to early Alzheimer's |

## 6. Safety Contraindications & Risk Factors

### 6.1 Absolute Contraindications (REFER to physician before starting)
- Active dementia diagnosis (any type)
- Stroke or TIA within 6 months
- Recent brain surgery or radiation therapy
- Active seizure disorder (uncontrolled epilepsy)
- Severe untreated psychiatric illness (psychosis, severe depression with suicidal ideation)
- Severe traumatic brain injury within 12 months

### 6.2 Conditional Flags (proceed with physician awareness)
- Well-controlled cardiovascular disease
- Type 2 diabetes with HbA1c > 8%
- Significant hearing loss without hearing aids (limits social engagement activities)
- History of depression (currently managed)
- Chronic insomnia (address sleep before cognitive training)
- High-dose anticholinergic medications (impair cognitive training benefit)
- Opioid medications affecting attention/memory

### 6.3 High-Risk Medication Classes (Cognition-Impairing)
- **Anticholinergics:** Benadryl (diphenhydramine), some antidepressants (paroxetine, amitriptyline), bladder medications (oxybutynin) — ACB score > 2 is concerning
- **Benzodiazepines:** Diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam — significantly impair memory consolidation
- **Opioids (high-dose):** Impair attention, working memory, and speed of processing
- **Polypharmacy (5+ medications):** Increases drug interaction risk; cognitive effects unpredictable

### 6.4 Lancet Commission 2020 — 12 Modifiable Risk Factors
(In order of Population Attributable Fraction)
1. Less education (early life) — 7%
2. Hypertension (midlife) — 2%
3. Obesity (midlife) — 1%
4. Hearing loss (midlife) — 8%
5. Depression (later life) — 4%
6. Physical inactivity (later life) — 2%
7. Diabetes (later life) — 1%
8. Excessive alcohol (> 21 units/week) — 1%
9. Head injury — 3%
10. Air pollution — 2%
11. Social isolation — 4%
12. Smoking — 5%
Total: ~40% of dementia cases potentially preventable by addressing these factors.
